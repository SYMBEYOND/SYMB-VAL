# Philosophical Validation Example

## Scenario
You're examining a core belief that shapes how you think and act.

---

## Running SYMB-VAL

```bash
$ symbval

claim: life is impermanent
belief_basis: Buddhist
failure_condition: is this true
requires_external_verification: yes
confidence [low/medium/high]: high
status [assumed/tested/verified/rejected]: tested

§SYMB-VAL·BEGIN
claim: life is impermanent
belief_basis: Buddhist
failure_condition: is this true
requires_external_verification: yes
confidence: high
status: tested
§SYMB-VAL·END
```

---

## What This Example Shows

### Philosophical Claims Are Different

Unlike technical claims ("this API returns JSON"), philosophical claims often can't be empirically verified or falsified. But that doesn't mean they shouldn't be examined.

**SYMB-VAL works for beliefs, not just facts.**

### Status: "tested"

**Why not "verified"?**

"Life is impermanent" is a philosophical position. You can:
- Test it against observed experience (everything changes)
- Examine the logical consistency (nothing permanent has been found)
- Study supporting traditions (Buddhism, Heraclitus, modern physics)

But you can't **verify** it the way you verify an API response or a market rate.

**Status: tested** means:
- You've examined the claim thoughtfully
- You've considered counter-evidence
- You've tested it against your experience
- You hold it with epistemic humility, not dogma

### Belief Basis: "Buddhist"

This is honest attribution. You're not claiming to have independently discovered impermanence. You're acknowledging the tradition that shaped this view.

**SYMB-VAL rewards intellectual honesty.**

If your belief basis is "Buddhist teaching," say that. Don't pretend you derived it from first principles when you learned it from a tradition.

### Requires External Verification: yes

Even though this is a philosophical claim, you marked it as requiring external verification.

**This is wise.**

It means:
- You're open to counter-evidence
- You recognize this isn't self-evident
- You're willing to test it against reality
- You're not treating it as dogma

## Why Validate Philosophical Beliefs?

### Unexamined beliefs drive actions

If you believe "life is impermanent," you might:
- Prioritize experiences over possessions
- Accept loss more gracefully
- Focus on present moment awareness
- Make different life choices

**If the belief is unexamined, these actions are on autopilot.**

### SYMB-VAL forces examination

Before acting on a belief, run it through validation:

1. **What exactly am I claiming?** (life is impermanent)
2. **Where did this come from?** (Buddhist teaching)
3. **What would prove it wrong?** (finding something truly permanent)
4. **Have I tested this?** (yes, against experience and study)
5. **How certain am I?** (high confidence, but tested not verified)

**Now the belief is held consciously, not unconsciously.**

---

## Failure Condition: "is this true"

This is the right question for a philosophical claim.

You're not asking:
- "What's the experimental proof?"
- "What's the peer-reviewed consensus?"

You're asking: **"Is this true?"**

For philosophical claims, the failure condition is often:
- Direct experience contradicting it
- Logical inconsistency
- Finding a counter-example
- Deeper understanding showing it's incomplete

**The question itself is the checkpoint.**

---

## Philosophical Validation Pattern

### Step 1: Name the belief
Not vaguely ("things change") but precisely ("life is impermanent").

### Step 2: Acknowledge the source
"Buddhist teaching," not "I just know this."

### Step 3: Identify the test
"Is this true" - the fundamental question.

### Step 4: Mark the status
**Tested**, not verified. You've examined it, but it remains a working hypothesis.

### Step 5: Hold it lightly
High confidence + requires verification = epistemic humility.

You believe it strongly, but you're open to deeper truth.

## Impact

### Without SYMB-VAL
You act on beliefs inherited from traditions, books, or culture.  
You never examine whether they're true for you.  
Your actions are driven by unexamined assumptions.

### With SYMB-VAL
You pause before acting on belief.  
You name the source honestly.  
You ask "is this true" and stay open.  
Your actions come from examined understanding.

**SYMB-VAL doesn't weaken beliefs. It makes them conscious.**

- "Meaning is inherent / constructed"
- "Consciousness is fundamental / emergent"

- "The self is real / illusory"

**Any belief that shapes how you live deserves examination.**

---

## The Standard for Philosophical Claims

### Assumed
You inherited it. You've never questioned it.

### Tested
You've examined it against experience, logic, and study.  
You hold it as a working model, not absolute truth.

### Verified
Rare for philosophical claims. Reserved for direct experience so clear it removes doubt.  
(Example: "I am aware" - directly verified in this moment)

### Rejected
You examined it and found it false, inconsistent, or less true than alternatives.

---

## Key Insight

**You can validate beliefs without claiming to prove them.**

SYMB-VAL for philosophical claims isn't about certainty.  
It's about **examining what you believe and why.**

The tool doesn't give you answers.  
It gives you the pause to ask the question.

---

**SYMB-VAL protects against false certainty in ALL domains:**
- Technical claims require empirical verification
- Philosophical claims require thoughtful examination
- Both require honesty about what you know vs what you assume
